Southeast Asian Haze Crisis Dashboard

Comprehensive Analysis of Transboundary Haze Pollution in Malaysia and Regional Impact Assessment

Executive Summary

Transboundary haze pollution has been a recurring environmental crisis in Southeast Asia since 1972, with the most severe episodes occurring in 1997 and 2015. The primary source is peat fires in Indonesia, particularly in Sumatra and Kalimantan, caused by slash-and-burn land clearing for palm oil plantations.

Historical Timeline of Major Haze Episodes

1972

First recorded transboundary haze episode in Southeast Asia

1997-1998

Most severe episode on record. Affected area: 3+ million km². Economic damage: $9 billion across ASEAN. Malaysia declared state of emergency in Sarawak. 40,000+ hospitalizations across the region.

2002

ASEAN Agreement on Transboundary Haze Pollution signed in Kuala Lumpur

2005

Significant haze event leading to widespread school closures in Malaysia. State of emergency declared at Port Klang with API above 500.

2013

Highest Air Pollutant Index (API) readings ever recorded in Malaysia, state of emergency declared in several regions

2015

Second most severe episode. Indonesia alone: $16.1 billion in damages. 2.6 million hectares burned. 100,000+ estimated deaths. Singapore Air Quality Index reached hazardous levels for weeks.

2019

Return of significant haze after 3-year respite. $5 billion estimated costs. 850,000+ hectares burned in Indonesia.

2023-2024

Moderate episodes with increasing hotspots. Open burning cases surge: Kedah (741), Selangor (661), Johor (505), Sabah (534)

2025

Current Status: Medium risk assessment by Singapore Institute of International Affairs. Elevated agricultural commodity prices increasing deforestation pressure.

Sector-Specific Economic Losses

Healthcare Sector Burden

Annual inpatient costs: $91,000 USD per year in Malaysia (Kuala Lumpur-Selangor region). Healthcare utilization increase: 31% increase in inpatient cases during haze episodes. Regional pattern: Sarawak recorded highest inpatient rates during severe episodes.

Tourism Industry Impact

Flight cancellations: Regular disruptions during peak episodes. Visitor arrivals: Significant decline during haze seasons. Visit Malaysia 2026: Government targeting 23.5 million tourists, RM 76.8 billion revenue - haze poses major risk.

Agriculture and Palm Oil

Crop productivity: Reduced yields during haze periods. Palm oil production: 8.3% decline in 1998 due to El Niño and haze conditions. Fisheries damage: Coastal areas affected by air quality degradation.

Manufacturing and Services

Industrial production: Factory shutdowns during severe episodes. Supply chain disruption: Port and logistics delays. Energy sector: Power generation efficiency affected by air quality.

Root Causes of Haze Pollution

Primary Causes

1. Peatland Destruction and Drainage

Indonesia has 265,500 km² of peatland (13.9% of land area). Malaysia has 26,685 km² (8.1% of land area). Over 60% of peat swamp forests converted or degraded in past 20-30 years. Drained peat becomes extremely fire-prone due to high carbon content.

2. Slash-and-Burn Agriculture

Industrial-scale land clearing for palm oil and pulpwood plantations. Fire used as cheapest and fastest method compared to mechanical clearing. 40% of all fires in 2009 detected in peatlands despite covering only 10% of studied area.

3. Palm Oil Industry Expansion

Indonesia and Malaysia produce 85% of world's palm oil. Indonesia: 11.9 million hectares under cultivation, projected to reach 13 million by 2025. Between 2009-2011, 25% of Indonesian forestland cleared for palm plantations.

Corporate Responsibility and Involvement

Malaysian and Singaporean Companies in Indonesia

Major companies involved: IOI Corporation, Genting, KLK (Malaysia); Bumitama, Musim Mas (Singapore). Many fires occurred on land owned by Malaysian and Singaporean plantation companies. Complex ownership structures often obscure true accountability.

Contributing Factors

Meteorological Factors

  • El Niño weather patterns causing extended dry seasons
  • Monsoon delays reducing natural fire suppression
  • Wind patterns carrying smoke across borders
  • Climate change intensifying dry conditions

Economic and Governance Factors

  • Weak law enforcement in Indonesia
  • Patronage networks protecting palm oil companies
  • Economic incentives favoring rapid land clearing
  • Limited accountability for transboundary pollution

Government Initiatives and Response

Malaysian Government Actions

Policy Framework

Environmental Quality Act (1974): Foundation of Malaysia's environmental policy

Twelfth Malaysia Plan (2021-2025): Focus on advancing green growth and environmental sustainability

National Energy Transition Roadmap (NETR): Launched July 2023, targeting net-zero emissions

Current Government Efforts (2024-2025)

  • Proposed Clean Air Act - expected presentation to Parliamentary Committee in 2024
  • Enhanced monitoring through 65 air quality stations nationwide
  • Cloud seeding operations during severe episodes
  • Diplomatic engagement with Indonesia on transboundary fires
  • ASEAN Chairmanship 2025 focusing on environmental rights

Regional Cooperation Efforts

ASEAN Agreement on Transboundary Haze Pollution (2002)

Signed in Kuala Lumpur, ratified by Indonesia in 2014. Limited enforcement mechanisms have resulted in continued annual episodes despite agreement.

Singapore's Transboundary Haze Pollution Act (2014)

Criminalizes activities overseas contributing to haze in Singapore. Malaysia considered similar legislation but shelved in 2020.

Indonesian Government Response

Peatland Restoration Agency (BRG)

Established 2015 post-haze crisis. Mandate: restore and conserve peat forests, block drainage canals, rewet peatlands. 12,000+ wells drilled for firefighting by 2018.

Law Enforcement

203 companies warned in 2024, 20 companies sealed including Malaysian subsidiaries. Legal actions against violators, though enforcement remains challenging.

Political and Media Response

Government Statements and Actions

Minister Nik Nazmi called for regional ASEAN action on transboundary haze. Malaysia "seriously considering" laws holding companies liable for air pollution. Diplomatic notes sent to Indonesian counterparts regarding current fires.

Public Criticism and Accountability

Increasing public criticism despite media controls. Social media campaigns demanding stricter enforcement. Environmental groups filing human rights complaints. Growing awareness of health impacts driving policy pressure.

Religious and Cultural Perspectives

Religious Values and Environmental Protection

Mixed responses from Malaysian religious communities. Some disconnect between religious teachings and environmental action. Government-sanctioned prayers for rain during episodes. Emphasis on scientific solutions over divine intervention.

Human Rights Recognition

UN Resolution on Environmental Rights (2022)

Malaysia among 161 nations voting for historic resolution declaring clean, healthy, sustainable environment as universal human right. Resolution strengthens civil society demands for clean air through Malaysian Human Rights Commission.

Challenges in Funding Distribution

Enforcement and Implementation Gaps

Despite significant allocations, enforcement remains weak. Third-sector organizations report insufficient sustained funding for meaningful long-term solutions. Need for enhanced coordination between government and non-government sectors.

Corporate Accountability Issues

Many financial institutions continue to fund companies linked to deforestation. Limited transparency in supply chain financing. Calls for stronger due diligence in environmental risk assessment.
